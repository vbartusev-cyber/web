About This Analysis
This stakeholder register was compiled in accordance with PMBOK 7th Edition principles and the Rand Water Engineering PMO governance framework. Each stakeholder was evaluated against two axes — power (ability to influence project outcomes) and interest (degree to which they are affected by the project) — and assigned to one of four engagement quadrants. The resulting communication strategies are proportionate, targeted, and designed to protect the project's regulatory standing, community licence to operate, and financial integrity throughout the 70-week execution window.

Strategy JustificationsWhy each quadrant
Manage Closely — High Power / High Interest
Active Collaboration
These stakeholders hold regulatory, financial, or operational authority over the project and stand to gain or lose significantly from its outcome. NERSA can revoke a licence; National Treasury can freeze funds; Eskom can deny grid access. Each of these outcomes would terminate the project. Daily or bi-weekly touchpoints, written confirmation of all approvals, and escalation procedures are essential to maintain their support and pre-empt surprises.
Keep Satisfied — High Power / Low Interest
Proactive Reporting
Funding institutions and investors hold financial power but are not operationally involved. Their primary concern is ESG compliance, loan repayment security, and risk visibility. Monthly financial reports and transparent communication about delays prevent disengagement or funding withdrawal. Over-involving them in technical decisions would be counterproductive; they need confidence, not complexity.
Keep Informed — Low Power / High Interest
Transparent Communication
Operations staff, local communities, and field engineers are directly affected by day-to-day project activity. Though they hold limited formal power, their collective resistance can create delays through grievances, safety incidents, or negative media coverage. Regular briefings, a community feedback channel, and onboarding procedures convert potentially resistant stakeholders into advocates and protect the project's social licence.
Monitor — Low Power / Low Interest
Lightweight Oversight
Equipment suppliers and public housing associations have a peripheral relationship with the project. Disproportionate engagement would waste project manager bandwidth. Quarterly short-form updates and prompt payment procedures are sufficient. However, this group should be re-evaluated at each phase gate — a supplier dispute or community association concern can escalate their interest level rapidly if left unaddressed.
